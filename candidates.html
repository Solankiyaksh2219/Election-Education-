🏛️ Candidates & Parties

Learn how candidates are nominated, how parties work, and what symbols represent.

📋

How Candidates Are Nominated

The nomination process is a crucial step in elections:

  • A person must be a citizen of India and at least 25 years old (Lok Sabha) or 30 years old (Rajya Sabha)
  • Candidates file nomination papers with the Returning Officer
  • They must deposit ₹25,000 (₹12,500 for SC/ST candidates)
  • At least one registered voter must propose the candidate
  • Candidates must declare criminal cases, assets, and education
🏢

Role of Political Parties

Political parties are the backbone of representative democracy:

  • Parties select and field candidates for elections
  • They create manifestos outlining policies and promises
  • Parties campaign on behalf of their candidates
  • In Parliament, they form the ruling party or opposition
  • Parties must be registered with the Election Commission
🏅

National vs Regional Parties

India recognizes different categories of parties:

  • National Party: Must secure 6% votes in 4+ states OR win 2% Lok Sabha seats from 3+ states
  • State Party: Must secure 6% votes and win 2 seats in a state assembly election
  • Registered Unrecognized: Registered but don't meet national/state criteria
  • Currently India has 6 national parties and 50+ state parties
👤

Independent Candidates

You don't need a party to contest elections:

  • Any eligible citizen can contest as an independent candidate
  • Must be proposed by 10 registered voters of the constituency
  • The ECI assigns a unique "free symbol" from its reserved list
  • Independents have won and become ministers in the past
  • No party support means self-funded campaigns
🤝

Coalitions & Alliances

When no single party wins a majority, coalitions form:

  • Pre-election alliances: Parties agree to share seats before elections
  • Post-election alliances: Formed after results to reach the majority mark
  • A coalition needs 272+ seats to form government at the center
  • Common Minimum Programme binds coalition partners
⚖️

Anti-Defection Law

Protects the stability of elected governments:

  • Enacted in 1985 under the 52nd Amendment (10th Schedule)
  • An elected member is disqualified if they voluntarily leave their party
  • Voting against the party whip leads to disqualification
  • Exception: If 2/3 of the party's legislators agree to merge
  • Speaker of the House decides on disqualification petitions
